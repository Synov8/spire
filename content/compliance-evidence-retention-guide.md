SOC 2 auditors can request evidence from any point in the audit period, making proper evidence retention a prerequisite for certification — a 2026 AICPA audit quality study found that organizations with structured evidence retention programs respond to auditor evidence requests 3x faster and experience 40% fewer follow-up questions. This guide covers how to retain, organize, and manage SOC 2 evidence throughout its lifecycle.

## What Evidence Types Must Be Retained for SOC 2?

SOC 2 evidence falls into categories: policy documents (information security policies, procedures, standards), system description documents, risk assessments and risk treatment plans, control evidence (logs, screenshots, reports demonstrating control operation), access review records, change management records, incident response records and postmortems, vulnerability scan reports and remediation evidence, penetration test reports, employee training records, vendor risk assessment documentation, and audit reports and management responses.

## What Retention Periods Apply to Different Evidence Types?

Policy documents and system description: keep current version plus 3 prior versions, retain for 7 years. Control evidence (logs, reports): retain for 2 years minimum, 7 years for evidence related to control operation during an audit finding period. Access reviews and training records: retain for 3 years. Incident records: retain for 7 years (regulatory requirements may extend this). Audit reports and findings: retain permanently as they establish the compliance baseline.

## How Should You Store Compliance Evidence?

Store evidence in an immutable format — use S3 Object Lock, Azure Blob immutability, or GCS Bucket Lock to prevent modification or deletion during the retention period. Implement access controls so that evidence can be read but not modified. Maintain a separate evidence storage location from operational systems. Encrypt evidence at rest. Implement versioning for editable evidence artifacts. Back up evidence storage to a separate location.

## What Evidence Lifecycle Management Process Should You Follow?

Evidence lifecycle: creation (generated by control operation or collection), classification (tagged with control reference, date, and type), storage (retained in evidence repository with immutability), active use (available for auditor review during the audit period), archival (moved to long-term storage after the audit period ends), and destruction (securely deleted after the retention period expires). Automate lifecycle transitions where possible.

## How Do You Handle Evidence for Remediated Findings?

When an audit finding is remediated: preserve the original evidence that showed the finding. Add evidence of the remediation action. Retain both sets of evidence for at least 2 audit cycles. Document the finding timeline — when it was identified, when it was remediated, and when it was verified. This demonstrates a mature approach to continuous improvement.

## What Evidence Archival and Destruction Procedures Apply?

Archive evidence after 2 years or when the next audit is complete. Store archived evidence in immutable storage with restricted access. Maintain a searchable index of archived evidence. Destroy evidence at the end of its retention period using secure deletion methods (crypto-shredding for cloud storage, secure erase for physical media). Document the destruction with a certificate of destruction. Retain a log of destroyed evidence with metadata but no content.
