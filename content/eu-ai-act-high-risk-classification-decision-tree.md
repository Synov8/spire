The EU AI Act's high-risk classification framework under Article 6 determines whether an AI system triggers the Act's most stringent obligations — risk management, data governance, technical documentation, transparency, human oversight, and conformity assessment. The European Commission estimates that approximately 15% of AI systems deployed in the EU will qualify as high-risk, with B2B SaaS AI features most commonly falling under Annex III categories 1 (biometrics), 5 (employment and worker management), 6 (essential services), and 8 (administration of justice).

## What Is the Two-Step High-Risk Classification Test?

Step 1 asks whether the AI system is a safety component of a product covered by EU harmonization legislation listed in Annex II OR falls into one of the eight high-risk categories listed in Annex III. Step 2 asks whether the AI system poses a significant risk of harm to health, safety, or fundamental rights.

An AI system that passes Step 1 (Annex III category) but fails Step 2 (no significant risk) is not classified as high-risk. The European Commission's 2026 implementing guidelines provide detailed criteria for the Step 2 assessment, including: whether the system affects legal or similarly significant decisions, whether individuals can opt out of AI decision-making, and whether the system involves vulnerable populations.

## Which Annex III Categories Apply to B2B SaaS?

Category 5 (employment, worker management, and access to self-employment) covers AI systems used for recruitment, worker evaluation, and task allocation — affecting any SaaS company offering HR or workforce management AI features. Category 6 (access to essential private services and essential public services and benefits) covers AI credit scoring, insurance pricing, and access to essential services.

Categories 7 (law enforcement), 8 (migration, asylum, and border control), and 9 (administration of justice) typically apply to SaaS companies selling to government entities. Category 1 (biometrics) applies to SaaS products with facial recognition or biometric categorization features.

## What Happens When an AI System Is Substantially Modified?

Article 43(4) requires a new conformity assessment for substantially modified high-risk AI systems. A substantial modification is any change that may significantly impact the AI system's compliance with the Act — including changes to the intended purpose, model architecture changes with material performance impact, training data source changes, and deployment context changes.

## What Are the Obligations for High-Risk Systems?

High-risk AI systems require risk management system (Article 9), training data governance (Article 10), technical documentation (Article 11), record-keeping and logging (Article 12), transparency and information provision (Article 13), human oversight (Article 14), accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity (Article 15), and conformity assessment (Article 43).

## FAQ

### Can a B2B SaaS company's AI system be classified as minimal risk?

Yes. Most B2B SaaS AI features — content generation, internal analytics, customer support automation — will be classified as minimal or limited risk, triggering only transparency obligations under Article 50.

### When must high-risk classification be determined?

High-risk classification must be determined before the AI system is placed on the market or put into service. Reclassification is required whenever the system undergoes a substantial modification.
