Social media platforms deploy AI at unprecedented scale — a 2026 European Commission platform regulation report found that the 19 largest platforms designated under the DSA collectively operate over 2,300 distinct AI systems for content moderation, recommendations, and advertising. The EU AI Act imposes specific obligations on many of these AI systems, particularly those classified as high-risk or those subject to transparency obligations. The AI Act and the DSA create a comprehensive regulatory framework for social media AI.

## What Social Media AI Systems Are High-Risk?

High-risk AI systems on social media include: AI used for biometric categorization of users, AI systems for user access to essential services (if social media access is essential), and AI in employment contexts (if used for recruitment through the platform). Most content moderation and recommendation AI currently falls outside high-risk classification but faces significant transparency obligations and, through the DSA, systemic risk management requirements.

## What Content Moderation AI Requirements Apply?

Content moderation AI faces transparency obligations: automated content removal decisions must be explainable to users. Users have the right to appeal AI-based content decisions. Platforms must report AI moderation accuracy metrics. AI must be trained on representative data to minimize biased content removal. Emotion recognition AI for content moderation faces restrictions. High-risk classification may apply to moderation AI that determines access to essential services.

## What Recommendation Algorithm Transparency Is Required?

Recommendation AI must comply with: the DSA requires at least one non-profiled option (chronological feed). AI Act requires transparency about AI-generated recommendations. Users must understand key parameters influencing their feed. Systemic risk assessments for large platforms must evaluate recommendation AI impact. AI models must be tested for amplification of harmful content. Users must have meaningful control over their recommendation experience.

## What Advertising Targeting AI Obligations Apply?

Ad targeting AI faces restrictions in the AI Act and DSA: profiling for targeted advertising based on sensitive data (race, religion, health, etc.) is restricted. Targeted advertising to minors is prohibited. Transparency requirements for AI-driven advertising. No manipulative AI advertising practices. Automated ad targeting decisions must be explainable. These restrictions apply alongside GDPR requirements for profiling and automated decision-making.

## What Deepfake and Synthetic Content Requirements Apply?

AI-generated content on social media (deepfakes, synthetic media) must be labeled as AI-generated. Platforms must implement technologies to detect and label AI-generated content. User-generated content using AI must be identifiable. Platforms must prevent malicious AI-generated content (misinformation, non-consensual intimate images). Generative AI systems used to create social media content must comply with the AI Act's training data transparency and copyright requirements.

## How Does the AI Act Coordinate with the Digital Services Act?

The DSA and AI Act create a complementary regulatory framework: DSA covers platform obligations generally, AI Act adds specific AI system requirements. DSA risk assessments for large platforms must include AI-specific risks. AI Act conformity assessments apply to high-risk AI independent of DSA obligations. DSA transparency reporting should include AI system performance metrics. The enforcement mechanisms differ — DSA through Digital Services Coordinators, AI Act through market surveillance authorities. Large platforms should coordinate compliance across both frameworks.

## What Enforcement Timeline Should Social Media Platforms Follow?

Immediately: audit AI inventory for prohibited practices (social scoring, biometric categorization). By August 2025: implement labeling for AI-generated content (deepfakes, synthetic media). Ensure chatbot transparency. By August 2026: full conformity assessment for any high-risk AI systems. DSA obligations are already in effect for designated large platforms. Social media companies should prioritize deepfake detection and labeling capabilities given the 2025 deadline.
