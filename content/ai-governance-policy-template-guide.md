An AI governance policy is the foundational document for managing AI risk across any organization developing or deploying AI systems. The AI governance policy should address the full AI lifecycle — from procurement and development through deployment, monitoring, and retirement — and align with the three major AI governance frameworks: ISO 42001, the EU AI Act, and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0). A 2025 Gartner survey found that 77% of organizations with an AI governance policy in place had avoided at least one AI-related regulatory incident.

## What Sections Must an AI Governance Policy Include?

The core sections are: purpose and scope (defining which AI systems the policy covers, including third-party AI tools embedded in SaaS products), AI risk classification framework (defining how AI systems are classified as prohibited, high-risk, limited-risk, or minimal-risk), AI ethics principles (fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy, and human oversight), AI system development and deployment procedures (design, testing, documentation, and approval requirements), and AI incident management (identification, escalation, and remediation of AI system failures).

Each section should reference the specific regulatory requirements it addresses. Section 3 (risk classification) should cite EU AI Act Annex III categories. Section 4 (ethics) should reference ISO 42001 Clause 6 (planning) and the NIST AI RMF core functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage).

## How Should AI Vendor Governance Be Addressed?

The policy must address AI systems procured from third-party vendors — pre-built AI APIs, embedded AI features in SaaS tools, and customized AI models developed by consultants. Each third-party AI system must undergo the same risk classification as internally developed systems. The vendor's AI governance practices must be evaluated during procurement due diligence.

A 2025 CSA report found that 43% of AI-related compliance incidents involved third-party AI systems, making vendor AI governance a critical policy component.

## What Documentation Does the Policy Require?

The policy should mandate an AI system inventory (all AI systems in use, classification, owner, and risk assessment status), AI risk assessments for each high-risk system, transparency documentation (disclosure mechanisms for each AI system), training records (AI literacy completion by role), and monitoring logs (ongoing AI system performance and fairness metrics).

## How Often Should the AI Governance Policy Be Reviewed?

The AI governance policy should be reviewed at least annually or whenever significant regulatory developments occur. Given the rapid evolution of AI regulation in 2025-2026, quarterly review of the regulatory alignment section is recommended for companies deploying AI systems in the EU market.

## FAQ

### Does every company using AI need a formal AI governance policy?

Any organization developing or deploying AI systems that could affect individuals or have regulatory implications should have an AI governance policy. The policy's complexity scales with the AI systems' risk classification and regulatory exposure.

### Who should approve the AI governance policy?

The AI governance policy should be approved by the board of directors or senior management. AI governance should be owned by a designated AI ethics officer or compliance lead with cross-functional authority.
