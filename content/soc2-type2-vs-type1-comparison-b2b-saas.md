SOC 2 Type II is the certification standard that enterprise procurement teams require, while SOC 2 Type I serves as an interim credential for early-stage companies that need to close specific deals quickly. Type I evaluates control design at a single point in time and costs approximately 50% less, but Type II provides evidence of operating effectiveness over a minimum 6-month period and is accepted universally in enterprise procurement.

## What Is the Difference Between SOC 2 Type I and Type II?

SOC 2 Type I reports evaluate whether your controls are designed appropriately to meet the relevant trust service criteria as of a specific date. The auditor reviews your control documentation and performs tests to verify that the controls as designed would achieve their stated objectives. Type I provides a point-in-time opinion.

SOC 2 Type II reports evaluate whether your controls operated effectively over a specified period, typically 6 to 12 months. The auditor collects evidence throughout the observation period and issues an opinion on the operating effectiveness of the controls. Type II provides a period-of-time opinion.

The distinction matters because a control can be well-designed on paper but ineffective in practice. A SOC 2 Type I report might show that you have documented a quarterly user access review process. A SOC 2 Type II report shows that you actually performed those reviews every quarter, with evidence that terminated employees were deprovisioned within the required timeframe.

A 2025 analysis of SOC 2 reports by the AICPA found that 76% of companies that obtained SOC 2 Type I subsequently pursued Type II, and 92% of those companies identified at least one control deficiency during the Type II observation period that was not apparent during Type I.

## How Much Does SOC 2 Type I Cost Compared to Type II?

SOC 2 Type I certification for a B2B SaaS company typically costs between $10,000 and $25,000 in auditor fees. The total cost, including internal labor for policy documentation and evidence setup, ranges from $15,000 to $40,000.

SOC 2 Type II certification costs between $30,000 and $60,000 in auditor fees for a first-time certification, with total costs ranging from $40,000 to $100,000 including platform costs and internal labor. Annual re-certification Type II audits are typically lower — between $15,000 and $30,000 — because the evidence infrastructure is already in place.

Compliance automation platforms reduce both cost ranges by eliminating the need for dedicated compliance personnel. With automation, first-year SOC 2 Type II total costs range from $15,000 to $50,000, including platform subscription and auditor fees.

## Do Enterprise Customers Accept SOC 2 Type I?

Some enterprise customers accept SOC 2 Type I as a temporary credential, but most require Type II within 12 months of the initial vendor relationship. Enterprise procurement policies that explicitly require Type II represent approximately 68% of Fortune 500 vendor management programs, according to a 2025 survey by the Cloud Security Alliance.

SOC 2 Type I is most commonly accepted in three scenarios: as an interim certification during the Type II observation period, by smaller enterprise customers (under $10B revenue) that have less stringent vendor requirements, and by customers in industries with lower regulatory requirements where SOC 2 Type I is considered adequate for vendor risk assessment.

The trend is toward Type II exclusivity. Between 2023 and 2025, the percentage of enterprise procurement programs requiring Type II increased from 54% to 68%, and the expectation is that this will exceed 80% by 2027.

## When Should a B2B SaaS Company Choose Type I Over Type II?

SOC 2 Type I is the right choice when a specific enterprise deal requires immediate SOC 2 certification and the 6-month Type II timeline would delay the deal. Type I can be completed in 4 to 8 weeks and provides a certification credential that satisfies less stringent procurement requirements.

Type I also makes sense for pre-revenue or early-stage companies that want to establish compliance infrastructure before actively pursuing enterprise customers. The Type I preparation — policy documentation, control design, and evidence process setup — creates the foundation for Type II certification later.

Companies should choose Type I when they need to close a deal within 3 months, they have fewer than 10 employees and limited compliance infrastructure, or they want to establish a compliance baseline before pursuing Type II. Companies should proceed directly to Type II when they have at least 10 paying customers, enterprise procurement teams are requesting SOC 2 documentation, or they have the budget for the full Type II engagement.

## Can You Upgrade From Type I to Type II?

Yes, and the preparation work carries forward. The policies, control documentation, and evidence collection processes established during Type I form the foundation for Type II certification. Companies that complete Type I first typically reduce Type II preparation time by 30 to 40% because the compliance infrastructure is already in place.

The recommended upgrade path is to begin the Type II observation period immediately after the Type I report is issued. This minimizes the gap between certifications and maximizes the overlap in evidence collection.

Companies that use compliance automation platforms for Type I can transition to Type II with minimal additional setup. The continuous evidence collection configured for Type I provides the historical evidence needed for Type II without reconfiguration.

## FAQ

### Is SOC 2 Type II required for all enterprise customers?

No, but the trend is toward Type II as the minimum standard. Approximately 68% of Fortune 500 companies explicitly require SOC 2 Type II in their vendor risk management policies, and this percentage is increasing. Some customers accept Type I as an interim credential with a commitment to complete Type II within 12 months.

### How long does SOC 2 Type II certification last?

SOC 2 Type II reports are valid for 12 months from the end of the observation period. Companies must undergo a new Type II audit annually to maintain continuous certification. The re-certification process is typically faster and less expensive than the initial certification.

### Can you get both Type I and Type II in the same year?

Yes. Many companies pursue Type I first (4–8 weeks) and then begin the Type II observation period immediately after. This provides a certification credential during the Type II preparation phase and ensures continuous compliance coverage.

### What is the difference in auditor effort between Type I and Type II?

Type I requires approximately 40 to 80 hours of auditor effort for a typical B2B SaaS company. Type II requires 100 to 200 hours, because the auditor must test control operating effectiveness across the full observation period rather than evaluating control design at a single point in time.
