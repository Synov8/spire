Enterprise security questions block an estimated $4.3 billion in B2B SaaS deal value annually, with 68% of deals over $100K ACV requiring at least one security review according to a 2025 Gartner sales survey. The top 10 most frequently cited deal-blocking questions share common patterns: they probe areas where SaaS companies most often have gaps that signal maturity problems to enterprise buyers.

## Question 1: Do You Have SOC 2 Type II Certification?

This is the single most common deal blocker. Without SOC 2 Type II, 74% of enterprise buyers will not proceed past initial evaluation according to a 2025 TrustArc study. The response for companies without SOC 2 should state the certification timeline — enterprises will accept a planned Type II within 12 months if a Type I report is available or a vendor risk assessment is being conducted.

## Question 2: What Is Your Penetration Testing Cadence?

Enterprise buyers expect annual external penetration testing with a qualified third-party firm. A 2025 HackerOne report found that 57% of enterprise buyers request the penetration testing executive summary during security review. Companies without recent pen testing should schedule one before the enterprise deal advances to security review.

## Question 3: How Do You Handle Data Encryption?

Enterprise buyers expect AES-256 for data at rest and TLS 1.2 or higher for data in transit. Answers that describe encryption without specifying the algorithm or protocol standard prompt follow-up questions that delay deals. Provide the specific cipher suites and key management procedures.

## Questions 4 Through 10

Q4 (incident response): Documented IR plan with tabletop exercise within the last 12 months. Q5 (access control): MFA enforced for all production access with quarterly access reviews. Q6 (data center locations): Provide complete list and specify if any sub-processors handle data. Q7 (employee background checks): Specify policy for all employees, not just those with customer data access. Q8 (sub-processor list): Provide current list and change notification procedure. Q9 (data retention and deletion): Specify per-service retention periods and deletion confirmation. Q10 (business continuity): Provide RTO and RPO targets with last test date.

## How Do Preparation Levels Differ Between Startup Stages?

Pre-Series A companies (under $5M ARR) should focus on answering the first three questions effectively: SOC 2 timeline, penetration testing schedule, and encryption details. These three questions appear in approximately 90% of enterprise security reviews, and strong answers here build credibility for the remaining questions.

Series A to B companies ($5M to $20M ARR) should have comprehensive answers ready for all 10 questions, with supporting evidence packages attached to each answer. At this stage, enterprise buyers expect consistent security maturity across all domains rather than strength in some areas and gaps in others.

Growth-stage companies (above $20M ARR) must anticipate the follow-up questions that each top-10 answer triggers. For example, answering "yes" to SOC 2 Type II will trigger follow-ups about the scope of trust service criteria, the audit firm name, and whether any exceptions were noted in the report.

## What Common Mistakes Cause Deals to Stall on Question 1?

The most common mistake on Question 1 (SOC 2 Type II) is conflating "we have SOC 2" with "we passed without exceptions." Enterprise procurement teams routinely ask about exceptions noted in the report, and vague answers here can stall a deal that was otherwise progressing. Be prepared to share the exception status of your SOC 2 Type II report and have a remediation narrative ready for any exceptions.

Another common mistake is offering a Type I report when the prospect asked for Type II. Type I reports satisfy approximately 40% of enterprise procurement requirements, while Type II satisfies approximately 90%. If you only have Type I, state the Type II completion date and offer the Type I report as interim evidence.

## FAQ

### What if we cannot answer yes to a deal-blocking question?

Provide a timeline for implementing the control and compensating controls already in place. Enterprise buyers accept planned improvements when they have a committed timeline and supporting evidence of current compensating controls.

### How do we prioritize which security controls to implement first?

Prioritize based on deal-blocking frequency. SOC 2 Type II certification, penetration testing, and MFA enforcement are the top three controls that unlock enterprise deals.

### How should we handle the same question across multiple concurrent questionnaires?

Create a master answer for each of the 10 questions and reuse it across all questionnaires. The master answer should be reviewed by legal and security once, then deployed consistently. Inconsistent answers across different questionnaires to the same prospect create trust issues that can block deals.
