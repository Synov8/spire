Selecting the right SOC 2 auditor is the single most consequential compliance decision a B2B SaaS company makes — the wrong auditor can double the cost, extend the timeline by months, and produce a report that enterprise customers question. A 2025 AICPA survey found that 37% of SOC 2 report users (enterprise procurement teams) rejected at least one report in the prior year because the issuing CPA firm lacked sufficient industry expertise or the report's scope was unclear.

## Should You Choose Big Four or a Boutique Firm?

Big Four firms (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) offer established brand recognition that enterprise procurement teams trust without question. SOC 2 Type II reports from Big Four firms are universally accepted. Costs range from $30,000 to $80,000 for a first-time SOC 2 Type II report for a startup with Security-only scope.

Boutique CPA firms offer lower costs ($10,000 to $30,000 for comparable scope), more partner attention (Big Four engagements often delegate fieldwork to junior staff), and specialized SaaS industry expertise. The trade-off is that some enterprise procurement teams may prefer Big Four reports, though boutique firms licensed by the AICPA produce equally valid reports. A 2025 Gartner report found that 91% of enterprise procurement teams accept boutique firm SOC 2 reports for mid-market deals.

## What Qualifications Must the Auditor Have?

The auditor must be a CPA firm licensed in the United States and registered with the AICPA, must have personnel with current SOC 2 training (AICPA SOC 2 training certification), must have SOC 2 experience with SaaS companies of similar size and scope, and must be independent — not your compliance platform, not your consulting firm, and not otherwise engaged in a prohibited non-audit service.

## What Questions Should You Ask Prospective Auditors?

Ask: How many SaaS SOC 2 Type II engagements did you complete in the last 12 months? What is your typical timeline from kickoff to final report for a company our size? How much partner time versus senior associate time will our engagement receive? What is your approach to controls that fail testing? Do you accept automated evidence collection as sufficient? What is the post-report support process?

## What Is the SOC 2 Auditor RFP Process?

The RFP process should include: developing a scope document (trust service criteria, systems in scope, number of controls), sending the RFP to 3 to 5 qualified firms, scheduling chemistry calls with the proposed engagement teams, requesting and checking references from similar SaaS companies, and comparing proposals on a weighted evaluation matrix covering qualifications, approach, timeline, cost, and cultural fit.

## What Should the Audit Engagement Letter Include?

The engagement letter from the chosen CPA firm should specify: the trust service criteria in scope (Security, Availability, Confidentiality, Processing Integrity, Privacy), the system description boundaries (which systems and processes are included), the Type II report format (SOC 2 examination report with auditor's opinion), the fieldwork timeline (when the auditor will be on-site or remote), the deliverables (draft report, management representation letter, final report), the fee structure (fixed fee or time-and-materials, travel costs, out-of-pocket expenses), and the timeline for report delivery after fieldwork completion.

Review the engagement letter carefully for scope limitations. Some firms exclude specific controls or systems that should be in scope for your enterprise customers. The engagement letter should also specify the report issuance timeline — standard industry practice is 4 to 8 weeks from fieldwork completion to final report delivery.

## What Common Auditor Selection Mistakes Should You Avoid?

The three most common selection mistakes are: choosing based on cost alone (the cheapest auditor may lack SaaS experience, producing a report that enterprise customers question), failing to check references from similar SaaS companies (auditor quality varies significantly within the same firm), and changing auditors between Type I and Type II without proper transition planning (continuity issues can delay the Type II report).

A fourth mistake is selecting an auditor who does not accept automated evidence collection. Some traditional CPA firms still expect manual screenshots and signed attestations for controls that modern compliance platforms automate. Verify the auditor's stance on automated evidence before signing the engagement letter.

## FAQ

### Can we use the same firm that does our financial audit?

Yes, provided the audit team is independent from the financial audit team and appropriate safeguards against self-review threats are documented. Many companies use the same firm for both audits.

### How far in advance should we book the auditor?

Book the auditor 3 to 6 months before the observation period end date. Quality boutique firms often book 3 to 4 months ahead. Big Four firms may require 4 to 6 months lead time.

### What questions should we ask the auditor's references from SaaS companies?

Ask: Did the auditor meet the committed timeline? Were fieldwork requests reasonable and organized? Did the auditor provide actionable feedback on control gaps? Were there unexpected fees or scope changes? Would you use this auditor again?
