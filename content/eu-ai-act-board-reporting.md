The EU AI Act imposes direct obligations on organizational leadership — a 2026 EMEA board governance survey found that 67% of EU company boards now have AI compliance as a standing board agenda item, with 42% establishing dedicated AI committees. While the AI Act primarily addresses providers and deployers of AI systems, boards have an indirect but significant responsibility for ensuring their organizations comply. This guide covers EU AI Act reporting and governance obligations for board members.

## What Are the Board's AI Act Responsibilities?

Board responsibilities include: ensuring the organization has adequate AI governance structures in place, approving AI risk management frameworks, overseeing high-risk AI system deployment, ensuring adequate resources for AI Act compliance, monitoring AI compliance metrics and reporting, approving EU declaration of conformity for high-risk AI systems (where applicable), and understanding liability exposure from AI systems. Boards should treat AI Act compliance as analogous to data protection, cybersecurity, or financial compliance oversight.

## What AI Governance Structures Should Boards Establish?

Recommended governance structures: board-level AI committee or assign AI oversight to an existing committee (audit, risk, or technology committee). Executive-level AI officer (Chief AI Officer or similar) responsible for day-to-day AI governance. Cross-functional AI compliance working group including legal, compliance, engineering, data science, and risk management. AI ethics advisory board for high-risk AI systems. Regular management reporting on AI compliance status.

## What AI Act Information Should Boards Receive Regularly?

Quarterly board reporting should include: AI inventory summary (count of AI systems by risk classification), high-risk AI system deployment status, AI incident reports and trends, conformity assessment progress (where applicable), regulatory developments and enforcement actions, AI compliance investment and resource needs, and material AI risks and mitigation status. Boards should also receive annual AI compliance reports covering the full AI Act compliance program.

## How Should Boards Approve AI Conformity Declarations?

For high-risk AI systems requiring EU declaration of conformity: board (or delegated committee) should review and approve the declaration. Approve only after confirming: risk management system is operational and documented, technical documentation is complete and accurate, conformity assessment has been conducted (internal or third-party), quality management system is in place, and post-market monitoring system is established. Document board approval in meeting minutes. Understand that signing the declaration carries legal liability.

## What Liability Exposure Do Boards Face Under the AI Act?

The AI Act does not create direct personal liability for board members, but: corporate penalties for non-compliance can reach 7% of global annual turnover for prohibited practices, 3% for other violations. Directors' duties under national company law may require oversight of AI compliance. The AI Liability Directive (proposed) may create direct liability for AI-related harm. D&O insurance should be reviewed for AI liability coverage. Board minutes should document AI Act compliance oversight to demonstrate good governance.

## How Should Boards Prepare for AI Act Enforcement?

Board preparation steps: conduct an AI Act readiness assessment at the board level. Establish AI governance charter defining board and management responsibilities. Review D&O insurance coverage for AI-related liabilities. Ensure AI compliance is included in the risk management framework. Engage external AI governance expertise for board education. Monitor regulatory developments through industry associations and legal advisors. Scheduled board AI Act reporting should begin at least 12 months before applicable deadlines.
