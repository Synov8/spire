SOC 2 compliance is not a one-time project — it requires continuous operation because the Type II report is valid for only 12 months from the end of the observation period. The annual re-certification cycle requires new evidence collection, updated policy reviews, and a new auditor engagement every year. Companies that treat SOC 2 as a project rather than an ongoing program are the ones that scramble for evidence 60 days before their report expires and end up with a gap in compliance coverage.

## What Does Ongoing SOC 2 Compliance Look Like?

Ongoing SOC 2 compliance operates on three cadences. Daily and weekly automated evidence collection runs continuously through your compliance platform — cloud infrastructure configurations, identity provider user lists, and code repository security settings are collected on a recurring schedule without human intervention.

Monthly tasks include reviewing automated control monitoring alerts, investigating configuration drifts flagged by the platform, and updating vendor risk records if vendor SOC reports have been received. Quarterly tasks include performing user access reviews, reviewing and updating security policies, conducting employee security training, and reviewing incident response logs.

Annual tasks — the heaviest lift — include the re-certification Type II audit, penetration testing, comprehensive vendor risk reassessment, policy review and approval, and business continuity plan testing.

Companies using compliance automation report spending 3 to 5 hours per week on ongoing SOC 2 maintenance, compared to 10 to 15 hours per week for manual programs. The 2025 AICPA study found that automated continuous monitoring reduced evidence gaps by 83% compared to periodic collection.

## How Do You Handle the Annual SOC 2 Re-Certification?

The annual re-certification audit follows the same process as the initial certification but is typically faster and less expensive. The auditor reviews the new 12-month observation period evidence, tests a sample of controls, and issues a new Type II report. Re-certification audit fees are typically 30% to 40% lower than the initial audit because the auditor is familiar with your environment.

The re-certification timeline should be planned to avoid a gap in compliance coverage. Start the new observation period immediately after the previous report's end date. Begin auditor engagement 3 to 4 months before the current report expires. The typical re-certification fieldwork takes 2 to 4 weeks, with the report issued 2 to 3 weeks after fieldwork closes.

Companies using continuous compliance monitoring can extend their observation period seamlessly because evidence is collected every day, not just during the formal observation window. The 12-month observation period is a minimum — many companies run a continuous 12-month rolling observation window for Type II with an annual fieldwork cycle.

## What Happens If Your SOC 2 Report Lapses?

A lapsed SOC 2 report — a gap between the expiration of one report and the issuance of the next — creates a compliance gap that enterprise procurement teams may flag during vendor due diligence. Some customers will continue to work with you during a short gap if a new report is in progress. Others will put new deals on hold until the fresh report is issued.

The practical risk of a lapsed report is deal velocity, not immediate disqualification. Most enterprise procurement teams accept a bridge letter — a letter from your auditor confirming that the new audit is in progress — as interim evidence of ongoing compliance. However, some customers have policies requiring a current SOC 2 report on file, and a gap of more than 60 to 90 days may trigger procurement holds.

The safest approach is to never let the report lapse. Start the re-certification observation period before the current report expires and schedule fieldwork to complete before the report end date. Continuous evidence collection makes this straightforward because there is no gap in available evidence.

## How Do You Maintain Evidence Collection Year-Round?

Continuous evidence collection is the foundation of ongoing SOC 2 compliance. The compliance platform collects evidence from connected integrations every day or week, stores it with timestamps and cryptographic integrity verification, and alerts the team when evidence collection fails or configuration drifts outside policy boundaries.

Day-to-day evidence management requires: monitoring evidence collection alerts daily, investigating failed collection runs within 24 hours, reviewing control drift notifications and remediating flagged issues, and maintaining the integration connections as your infrastructure evolves. Most compliance platforms send Slack or email notifications for collection failures and control drifts.

The evidence collected throughout the year becomes the foundation for the next re-certification audit. When the auditor requests evidence, it is already organized by control, time-stamped, and ready for review. Companies with mature continuous monitoring programs report re-certification fieldwork periods of 1 to 2 weeks — half the time of initial certification.

## How Often Should You Update Security Policies?

Security policies should be reviewed at least annually and updated whenever your infrastructure, team structure, or regulatory obligations change significantly. The annual review should coincide with the re-certification cycle so that policy updates are reflected in the new Type II observation period.

Common triggers for policy updates include new cloud provider or infrastructure adoption, team growth above 50 employees, new compliance framework adoption (adding ISO 27001 or HIPAA after SOC 2), regulatory changes (EU AI Act enforcement, new data privacy laws), and security incidents that reveal policy gaps.

Policy documents should include version history with approval dates and reviewer names. The auditor will check that current policies match current practices — a policy that describes a process your team no longer follows is worse than having no policy, because it demonstrates a control failure in P1.1 (accuracy and completeness of system description).

## FAQ

### How long does SOC 2 re-certification take?

SOC 2 re-certification typically takes 4 to 8 weeks from fieldwork kickoff to report issuance, compared to 6 to 12 weeks for initial certification. Companies with mature continuous evidence collection and no significant control changes can complete re-certification in 4 to 6 weeks.

### How much does SOC 2 re-certification cost?

Annual SOC 2 Type II re-certification audit fees range from $12,000 to $40,000, depending on auditor, company complexity, and scope. Re-certification is typically 30% to 40% less than the initial audit. Compliance platform subscriptions continue annually at the same rate.

### Can you switch auditors between certification cycles?

Yes. Switching auditors between cycles is common, especially when companies grow and need different auditor expertise or pricing. Request evidence export from the previous platform and provide the new auditor with the prior year's SOC 2 report for continuity.

### What is a SOC 2 bridge letter?

A bridge letter is a letter from your auditor confirming that the annual SOC 2 audit is in progress and that no material changes have been identified that would affect the previous report's conclusions. Bridge letters are accepted by most enterprise procurement teams as interim evidence during the re-certification period.
