SOC 2 auditors require 12 to 18 written policies mapped to the selected trust service criteria, and companies that draft all policies before the observation period begins reduce audit preparation time by 40% compared to those that write policies reactively during evidence collection. The AICPA's 2025 TSC mapping guidance confirms that every written policy must include version history, approval dates, and periodic review cadence to satisfy control design requirements under CC1.1, CC1.2, and CC5.1.

## What Are the Mandatory SOC 2 Policies?

The Information Security Policy is the foundational document — it defines the security program scope, roles, and responsibilities. Every SOC 2 audit requires this policy, and it must reference the specific trust service criteria in scope. The Acceptable Use Policy governs employee use of company systems and data. The Access Control Policy defines user provisioning, authentication requirements, and periodic access review procedures. The Change Management Policy covers all changes to production systems, including emergency changes and standard release procedures.

The Incident Response Policy defines incident classification, response procedures, and notification timelines. The Risk Assessment Policy documents the methodology for identifying and evaluating business risks. The Vendor Risk Management Policy defines third-party assessment and monitoring procedures. The Data Classification and Handling Policy specifies protection levels for each data sensitivity tier. The Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery Policy defines recovery objectives and plan testing cadence. The Encryption Policy specifies encryption standards for data at rest and in transit. The Physical Security Policy covers facility and data center access controls. The Employee Security Training Policy defines onboarding, annual, and role-specific training requirements.

## What Makes a Policy Auditor-Ready?

Auditor-ready policies include a version number and revision history table, approval signatures from the responsible executive (typically CEO or CISO), defined review cadence (at least annually), clear scope boundaries and exclusions, and specific references to the SOC 2 controls the policy supports. Policies should avoid generic language — instead of "access is reviewed periodically," specify "access reviews occur quarterly on the last business day of each quarter using automated identity provider reports."

## How Long Does Policy Development Take?

A startup with fewer than 25 employees typically requires 2 to 4 weeks to draft all 12 to 18 policies using templates. Policy customization — tailoring language to the company's specific infrastructure and processes — adds 1 to 2 weeks. Executive review and approval adds 1 week. Total policy development timeline: 4 to 7 weeks for a first-time SOC 2 candidate.

## FAQ

### Can I use open-source SOC 2 policy templates?

Yes. Open-source SOC 2 policy templates from SANS, NIST, and the Cloud Security Alliance provide solid starting points. The templates must be customized to your specific control environment — generic templates that are not tailored result in audit findings.

### Do policies need to be reviewed annually?

Yes. SOC 2 control CC1.4 requires periodic review of policies. Annual policy review is the standard cadence accepted by auditors. Document each review with a revision history entry.

### How are policies linked to SOC 2 controls?

Each policy should include a controls mapping table that lists which SOC 2 controls the policy supports. For example, the Access Control Policy supports CC6.1, CC6.2, CC6.3, and CC6.5.
