AI systems used in education — including student assessment and grading, learning analytics, admission decisions, and student monitoring — are classified as high-risk under the EU AI Act Annex III, affecting approximately 3,000+ education technology platforms serving EU educational institutions, according to a 2026 EU Commission market analysis. EdTech companies face the same stringent compliance timeline as HR tech, with additional considerations for children's data protection.

## What EdTech Systems Are High-Risk?

High-risk EdTech systems include AI-powered student assessment and automated grading, learning analytics that predict student performance or behavior, admission and enrollment decision AI tools, student monitoring and attention detection systems, personalized learning path recommendation engines, and AI proctoring and invigilation tools. Each of these systems directly impacts students' educational opportunities and outcomes.

## What Are the Unique EdTech Compliance Challenges?

EdTech companies face specific challenges including GDPR Article 8 compliance for children's consent (minimum 16 or 13 depending on member state), transparency obligations that must be understandable to students and parents, the prohibition of AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities of children, fundamental rights impact assessments that evaluate impact on minors' rights, and conformity assessment complexity given diverse EU education standards.

## What Compliance Documentation Does EdTech Need?

EdTech AI providers must maintain technical documentation including system design specifications and development methodology, training data descriptions including sources, scope, and preprocessing, model performance metrics including accuracy, fairness, and robustness testing results, risk management documentation identifying and mitigating risks to students, and human oversight procedures defining how educators retain control over AI-driven decisions.

## FAQ

### Does the EU AI Act ban AI proctoring in education?

The EU AI Act does not explicitly ban AI proctoring, but the high-risk classification requires that AI proctoring systems undergo conformity assessment, implement human oversight, and respect student privacy rights. Some member states may impose additional restrictions.

### Can EdTech companies use student data for AI training?

Student data used for AI training must comply with GDPR and the EU AI Act data governance requirements. Training data must be relevant, representative, free from errors, and address biases. Using student data without appropriate legal basis violates both GDPR and the AI Act.

### What is the enforcement timeline for EdTech?

Same as all high-risk systems: obligations effective August 2026, full conformity assessment and CE marking required by August 2027. EdTech companies serving EU markets should begin compliance preparation immediately.
