The EU AI Act's penalty structure imposes fines up to €35 million or 7% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher, for violations of prohibited AI practices. GPAI model non-compliance carries fines up to €15 million or 3% of annual global turnover. The enforcement timeline is phased, with GPAI fine enforcement beginning August 2, 2026, and high-risk AI system rules applying from December 2, 2027, following the AI Omnibus adjustment. For most B2B SaaS companies in limited or minimal risk categories, the most immediate enforcement mechanism is not direct regulatory action — it is European procurement teams requesting AI Act compliance documentation before approving vendor contracts.

## What Are the EU AI Act Penalty Tiers?

The AI Act establishes four penalty tiers based on the severity and nature of the violation. The highest tier applies to prohibited AI practices under Article 5 — violations carry fines of up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover. Prohibited practices include social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, and AI systems that manipulate behavior through subliminal techniques.

The second tier applies to non-compliance with high-risk AI system requirements under Articles 8 through 22 — violations carry fines of up to €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover. This includes failure to implement risk management, data governance, technical documentation, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, and cybersecurity requirements for high-risk systems.

The third tier applies to GPAI model non-compliance under Articles 53 and 55 — violations carry fines of up to €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover. GPAI providers who fail to produce required technical documentation, implement model safety measures, or comply with transparency and copyright obligations face this penalty tier. The GPAI Code of Practice was finalized in July 2025, and GPAI providers who sign it gain a presumption of conformity.

The fourth tier applies to supplying incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information to notified bodies or national competent authorities — violations carry fines of up to €7.5 million or 1.5% of global annual turnover.

## When Does Each Enforcement Phase Begin?

The AI Act's enforcement timeline has been significantly adjusted by the AI Omnibus proposal agreed in early 2026. The phased timeline is: prohibited AI practices (Article 5) effective February 2, 2025, AI literacy obligations (Article 4) effective February 2, 2025, GPAI model obligations (Articles 53 and 55) effective August 2, 2025, GPAI fine enforcement effective August 2, 2026, high-risk AI system rules for stand-alone Annex III systems effective December 2, 2027, and high-risk AI system rules for embedded Annex I products effective August 2, 2028.

The GPAI fine enforcement date of August 2, 2026, is the most imminent deadline for companies that develop or deploy general-purpose AI models. Providers must have technical documentation, transparency disclosures, and copyright compliance policies in place. Deployers using GPAI models via API should verify that their provider can supply the required information package before this date.

The December 2, 2027, date for stand-alone high-risk systems represents a significant delay from the original August 2026 timeline. The AI Omnibus adjustment gives companies an additional 16 months to prepare for high-risk system compliance.

## How Does EU AI Act Enforcement Actually Work?

The European AI Office has direct enforcement authority over GPAI providers. For high-risk AI systems, each EU member state designates national competent authorities — typically the national data protection authority or a designated AI authority — to enforce the Act within their jurisdiction.

The AI Office coordinates enforcement across member states through the European Artificial Intelligence Board (EAIB). The Board ensures consistent interpretation and application of the AI Act across all member states, similar to how the European Data Protection Board coordinates GDPR enforcement.

For US companies without an EU establishment, enforcement requires cooperation with US regulators or direct action through customer contractual requirements. In practice, the most immediate enforcement mechanism for US B2B SaaS companies is through European procurement — enterprise buyers increasingly require AI Act compliance documentation as part of their vendor risk assessment process.

A 2026 IAPP survey found that 73% of US technology companies selling to European customers had updated their compliance documentation to address EU AI Act requirements. The survey confirmed that procurement-driven compliance is the primary enforcement vector for US companies.

## What Compliance Documentation Do You Need to Avoid Penalties?

Companies should prepare documentation proportional to their AI Act risk classification. For limited and minimal risk AI systems, the minimum documentation is AI literacy training records for staff using AI tools (Article 4) and transparency disclosures for AI interactions (Article 50). For high-risk AI systems, documentation must include risk assessment and management records, technical documentation with system architecture and purpose, data governance and training data documentation, human oversight assignment records, and accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity testing evidence.

The AI Act requires that documentation be maintained for the lifespan of the AI system. Post-market monitoring records and serious incident reports should be retained as part of the compliance file. For GPAI providers, technical documentation per Annex XI and XII is required, including model architecture, training data sources, compute resources, and evaluation results.

## What Are the Most Common Compliance Gaps US SaaS Companies Face?

The most common EU AI Act compliance gaps for US SaaS companies fall into four categories. The documentation gap is the most frequent — companies have no AI system inventory and cannot provide a list of which AI models their product uses and where they are deployed. The transparency gap is second — chatbot interfaces and AI-generated content lack disclosure notices required by Article 50. The literacy gap is third — companies have no documented AI use policy or training records for staff. The vendor assessment gap is fourth — companies that use third-party AI APIs have not verified that their provider can supply the required compliance documentation.

Companies that close these four gaps before enforcement deadlines are significantly better positioned for European enterprise sales. Workstreet reported in May 2026 that US SaaS companies with complete EU AI Act documentation saw 40% faster European enterprise deal closure compared to those without.

## FAQ

### Can a US company be fined under the EU AI Act?

Yes. The AI Act applies extra-territorially to any organization that deploys AI systems where the outputs are used within the European Union. A US company with no EU office and no EU employees can be fined if European customers use its AI features and the company fails to meet applicable requirements.

### What is the difference between AI Act and GDPR penalties?

The AI Act's maximum penalty of €35 million or 7% of global turnover for prohibited practices is higher than the GDPR's maximum of €20 million or 4% of global turnover. The two regulations operate in parallel — an AI system that processes personal data must comply with both frameworks simultaneously.

### Does the AI Act require an EU representative for US companies?

The AI Act requires that providers and deployers established outside the EU appoint an authorized representative established in the EU before placing AI systems on the EU market. The authorized representative acts as a point of contact for EU authorities and can be held responsible for compliance documentation.

### Can complying with the GPAI Code of Practice prevent fines?

Signing the GPAI Code of Practice creates a presumption of conformity with Articles 53 and 55. Regulators will assume a GPAI provider complies with the Act's requirements unless evidence suggests otherwise. The Code functions as the closest available safe harbor in the AI Act framework for GPAI providers.
