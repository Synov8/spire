SOC 2 CC9.2 requires vendor risk management, and third-party audits are the most rigorous form of vendor oversight — a 2026 Gartner third-party risk survey found that organizations conducting vendor audits reduce vendor-related security incidents by 55% compared to those relying solely on vendor-provided documentation. However, audits must be proportional to risk. This guide covers third-party vendor audit management for SOC 2 compliance.

## How Should You Tier Vendors for Audit Frequency?

Tier 1 (critical vendors): vendors with access to customer data, hosting infrastructure, or critical business systems. Audit annually. Require SOC 2 Type II report. Conduct annual vendor security review. Tier 2 (important vendors): vendors integrated with your systems but without direct customer data access. Audit every 2 years. Require SOC 2 or equivalent report. Conduct biennial vendor security review. Tier 3 (low-risk vendors): vendors with no customer data access and no system integration. Tier 3 vendors need minimal oversight — collect SOC 2 reports if available but formal audit is optional.

## What Does a Vendor Audit Include?

Vendor audit scope: access controls and authentication, data protection and encryption, logging and monitoring, incident response capabilities, change management procedures, business continuity and disaster recovery, compliance certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001), and sub-processor management. For on-site audits, additionally inspect: physical security, employee security awareness, and security operations center. For remote audits: evidence review, video walkthroughs, and management interviews.

## How Do You Review a Vendor's SOC 2 Report?

When reviewing a vendor's SOC 2 report: verify the report covers the relevant period (must be within 12 months). Check the auditor opinion (unqualified/modified/qualified — unqualified is ideal). Review the system description to understand what's in scope. Check for any exceptions or findings. Review the trust service criteria covered (Security only vs all five). Assess whether any findings affect your use of the vendor. Document your review and decision.

## What Is the Vendor Audit Evidence Checklist?

Request from vendors: current SOC 2 Type II report, penetration test report (last 12 months), vulnerability scan results, incident response plan and recent incidents, business continuity test results, data handling and encryption documentation, sub-processor list and their certifications, and employee security training evidence.

## How Do You Handle Vendor Compliance Gaps?

When a vendor has compliance gaps: assess the risk to your organization and customer data, request a remediation plan with timeline, consider compensating controls (limit data shared, segment the vendor's access, enhance monitoring), set a deadline for remediation, re-assess after the deadline, and if remediation is not achieved, plan vendor replacement. Document all gap management decisions. Escalate significant vendor gaps to your risk management process.

## What Vendor Audit Evidence Should You Maintain?

Maintain for each vendor: vendor tier classification and rationale, current SOC 2 report or equivalent, SOC 2 report review documentation, vendor security assessment or audit results, vendor incident notification history (if any), vendor remediation plans and evidence, vendor contract sections on security, and vendor offboarding documentation (when applicable). Review your vendor evidence inventory quarterly and flag any vendors with expiring certifications or assessments.
