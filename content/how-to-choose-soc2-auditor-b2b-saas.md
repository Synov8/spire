Choosing the right SOC 2 auditor is the highest-leverage decision in the certification process. A boutique CPA firm specializing in SaaS startups will complete a Type I audit in 6 to 8 weeks for $8,000 to $18,000, while a Big Four firm may charge $30,000 to $100,000 for the same scope. The cheaper option is not always better — but for 84% of startup SOC 2 engagements, the boutique firm produces a report that enterprise procurement teams accept without question.

## Should You Choose a Boutique Firm or a Big Four Auditor?

Boutique CPA firms that specialize in technology startups are the right choice for most B2B SaaS companies below 200 employees. These firms focus exclusively on SOC 2 and ISO 27001 audits for cloud-native companies, understand modern infrastructure, and charge predictable fixed fees. Typical boutique Type I fees range from $8,000 to $18,000, and Type II fees range from $15,000 to $30,000.

Big Four firms (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) and large national firms are appropriate for companies above 500 employees, companies in heavily regulated industries (financial services, healthcare), or companies that have a large customer specifically requiring a Big Four auditor. Big Four audits cost $30,000 to $100,000+ and typically take longer because the audit team rotates and the firm uses standardized procedures designed for enterprise environments.

A 2025 Cloud Security Alliance survey found that 84% of enterprise procurement teams accept SOC 2 reports from boutique auditors. The remaining 16% specifically require Big Four reports — usually financial services companies or government contractors. Startups should ask their top 5 customers whether they accept boutique-firm SOC 2 reports before committing to a Big Four premium.

Mid-tier national firms (Grant Thornton, BDO, Moss Adams) offer a middle ground with fees of $15,000 to $35,000 for Type II audits. These firms are familiar to most enterprise procurement teams and provide more consistent quality than boutique firms, but at a higher price point.

## What Questions Should You Ask a SOC 2 Auditor Before Engaging?

Ask these five questions before signing an engagement letter: how many SOC 2 audits have you completed for SaaS companies with our employee count? What is your fixed fee for Security-only scope Type I and Type II? What is included in the fee — readiness assessment, walkthrough sessions, draft review iterations, final report delivery? How long do your SaaS audits typically take from fieldwork kickoff to report delivery? And do your reports include the full Trust Services Criteria description and management assertion?

The auditor's experience with your company size and tech stack matters more than their brand name. An auditor who has done 50 SOC 2 audits for 20-person companies on AWS will finish faster and cost less than a generalist auditor who primarily works with 500-person companies on on-premise infrastructure.

Get fixed-fee quotes from at least three firms. Fixed fees provide cost predictability. Hourly billing creates uncertainty because the auditor can extend fieldwork by requesting additional evidence — and the startup pays for each additional hour.

The SOC 2 audit process was analyzed in depth by Strac in June 2026, which confirmed that auditor experience with cloud-native infrastructure and SaaS control frameworks is the strongest predictor of audit timeline and cost.

## What Should a SOC 2 Engagement Letter Include?

The engagement letter is the contract between your company and the CPA firm. It should specify: the type of report (Type I or Type II), the trust service criteria in scope (Security only, or Security plus additional criteria), the observation period dates (for Type II), the fee structure (fixed fee preferred over hourly), the deliverables (SOC 2 report with management assertion, control description, and auditor opinion), and the timeline (readiness assessment dates, fieldwork dates, draft report date, final report date).

The engagement letter should also specify the evidence exchange process. Some auditors prefer direct access to the compliance automation platform for evidence review. Others request evidence manually. Confirming this process upfront prevents delays during fieldwork.

Most boutique firms will provide a draft engagement letter for review before signing. Review it carefully and ask about any unclear terms before committing.

## How Do You Prepare for the Audit Kickoff?

The audit kickoff meeting sets the tone for the entire engagement. Before the kickoff, ensure that your evidence collection system is producing consistent, time-stamped data for all controls in scope, your security policies are finalized and approved, you have identified the key personnel who will participate in auditor walkthroughs, and you have completed an internal readiness assessment and remediated identified gaps.

During the kickoff, the auditor will explain their evidence sampling methodology, walkthrough schedule, and fieldwork timeline. For Type II audits, the kickoff confirms the observation period start and end dates, the control testing approach, and the communication cadence during fieldwork.

Companies that prepare a pre-audit evidence package — organized by control with timestamps and source system attribution — report 30% to 40% shorter fieldwork periods because the auditor can begin testing immediately rather than requesting evidence iteratively.

## How Do You Handle Auditor Evidence Requests During Fieldwork?

During fieldwork, the auditor requests evidence samples to verify each control in scope. Evidence requests typically arrive as a list of specific control tests with required sample sizes — for example, the auditor requests 15 terminated employee deprovisioning records for a 6-month observation period.

Respond to evidence requests within 48 hours. Delays in evidence response are the most common cause of fieldwork timeline extensions. Use your compliance platform to locate the requested evidence quickly — automated evidence collection means the evidence is already time-stamped and organized by control.

If the auditor identifies a control gap, discuss it openly. Earlier discovery of a gap means more time to remediate before the report is issued. Attempting to hide or minimize gaps erodes trust with the auditor and may lead to a qualified opinion.

## FAQ

### How many SOC 2 auditors should I get quotes from?

Get quotes from at least three firms. Include one boutique, one mid-tier, and if your customers require it, one Big Four firm. Compare fixed fees, timelines, and audit approach before deciding.

### Can I use my compliance platform's recommended auditor?

Yes. Most compliance platforms have auditor partner networks with pre-established evidence access workflows. Using a platform-recommended auditor can reduce setup time because the evidence exchange process is pre-configured. However, verify that the recommended auditor's pricing is competitive with independent firms.

### What is the difference between a qualified and unqualified SOC 2 opinion?

An unqualified opinion means the auditor found controls to be designed and operating effectively — this is the clean report you want. A qualified opinion means the auditor identified one or more control deficiencies that are material to the overall SOC 2 report. Qualified opinions are accepted by some enterprise customers but may trigger additional procurement scrutiny.

### Do SOC 2 auditors require a penetration test?

Most SOC 2 auditors strongly recommend a penetration test as part of the SOC 2 engagement, and most enterprise customers require it alongside the SOC 2 report. The penetration test verifies that security controls are effective against real attack scenarios. Budget $5,000 to $15,000 for an annual penetration test from an independent firm.
