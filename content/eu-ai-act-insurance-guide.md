Insurance AI systems are among the most heavily regulated under the EU AI Act — a 2026 McKinsey insurance AI survey found that 62% of AI systems deployed by EU insurers will be classified as high-risk, with 28% potentially prohibited if they manipulate consumer behavior in insurance purchasing. The insurance sector's heavy reliance on automated decision-making for pricing, underwriting, and claims creates significant compliance obligations. This guide covers EU AI Act compliance for insurance companies.

## What Insurance AI Systems Are High-Risk?

High-risk AI systems in insurance include: insurance underwriting algorithms that determine coverage eligibility and premiums, health insurance pricing based on individual risk assessment, claims processing AI that automates claim validation and payment, fraud detection algorithms that flag claims for investigation, customer risk profiling, and AI-based insurance advice and product recommendations. Low-risk systems include customer service chatbots and general marketing AI.

## What Underwriting and Pricing AI Requirements Apply?

Underwriting AI systems must comply with: no discrimination based on protected characteristics (enforced by AI Act bias requirements plus existing insurance discrimination law), explainable AI features that can articulate why a specific risk classification or premium was assigned, technical documentation of risk models, training data quality and representativeness, human oversight of underwriting decisions, and transparency to consumers about AI-based pricing. Coordinate AI Act compliance with Solvency II model governance requirements.

## How Do Claims Processing AI Systems Comply?

Claims processing AI must ensure: fair and unbiased claim assessment across all policyholder demographics, transparent claim decisions with explanation to policyholders, human review of automated claim denials, accurate fraud detection without excessive false positives, data minimization in claim handling, and audit trails for all AI-based claim decisions. Claims AI must not create disproportionate barriers to legitimate claims — a prohibited practice if it could cause significant harm to consumers.

## What Solvency II Alignment Is Required?

The AI Act and Solvency II overlap in several areas: model governance — Solvency II requires model validation that satisfies AI Act technical documentation requirements. Risk management — ORSA frameworks should incorporate AI-specific risks. Data quality — both frameworks require high-quality, representative data. Human oversight — both require appropriate human involvement in automated decisions. Use your Solvency II compliance infrastructure as a foundation for AI Act compliance, extending it to cover AI-specific requirements.

## What Prohibited AI Practices Apply to Insurance?

Several prohibited AI practices are particularly relevant to insurance: social scoring by insurers (prohibited). Exploiting vulnerabilities of specific customer groups (prohibited — relevant for vulnerable customer segments). Manipulative AI that could cause significant harm (prohibited — relevant for insurance purchasing decisions). Biometric categorization of protected characteristics (prohibited). These prohibitions directly affect how insurers can use AI in customer interaction and risk assessment.

## What Enforcement Timeline Should Insurers Follow?

Insurance AI Act timeline: immediately — identify and prohibit any AI practices that would be banned. By August 2025 — ensure transparency for all customer-facing AI (chatbots, recommendations). By August 2026 — high-risk AI systems (underwriting, pricing, claims automation) must have completed conformity assessments, risk management, and technical documentation. Begin your AI inventory and risk classification now, as the 2026 deadline requires substantial preparation for complex insurance AI systems.
