Series A B2B SaaS companies should budget $30,000 to $80,000 for first-year SOC 2 Type II certification, with the exact cost depending on scope breadth, infrastructure complexity, and vendor choices. A 2025 Gartner survey of 200 Series A SaaS companies found that the median first-year SOC 2 cost was $48,000, and companies that budgeted at least $50,000 completed certification 2 months faster than those budgeting under $30,000 because they could invest in automation and expert guidance simultaneously.

## What Are the Key Budget Line Items?

The SOC 2 budget breaks into five categories: compliance automation platform ($7,500 to $15,000/year) covering evidence collection, control monitoring, and policy templates; SOC 2 audit fee ($15,000 to $30,000) covering the CPA firm's Type II report, which constitutes the largest single line item; penetration testing ($5,000 to $12,000) covering annual external testing by a qualified third party; security tool upgrades ($3,000 to $15,000) covering any additional tools needed to meet control requirements — endpoint protection, logging, or vulnerability scanning; and consulting or advisory ($5,000 to $25,000) covering optional gap analysis or policy development support.

## When Should You Start Spending?

The spending timeline should align with the certification timeline. Months 1 to 2: Budget $10,000 to $15,000 for compliance platform subscription and initial gap assessment. Months 3 to 5: Budget $5,000 to $12,000 for penetration testing and any security tool upgrades identified in the gap assessment. Months 6 to 7: Budget $10,000 to $15,000 for auditor booking deposit (typically 50% of audit fee). Months 8 to 9: Budget $5,000 to $15,000 for remaining audit fee and any remediation costs.

## How Should You Present the Budget to the Board?

Present SOC 2 as a revenue-enabling investment rather than a compliance cost. The budget request should include: the expected timeline for enterprise deal enablement (SOC 2 unlocks approximately 74% of enterprise procurement processes), the deal value at risk without certification (average $100K-$250K ACV per enterprise deal), and the ROI calculation showing certification cost recouped within the first 1 to 2 enterprise deals closed.

## How Can You Reduce First-Year SOC 2 Costs?

Cost reduction strategies include: Security-only scope (cheapest single criterion, reduces audit time by 30% compared to multi-criteria scope), compliance platform with strong automation (reduces consulting needs), penetration testing with a boutique firm ($3,000 to $7,000 versus $10,000 to $15,000 for larger firms), and scheduling the audit during the CPA firm's off-peak season (June to November) for potential rate negotiations.

## How Does Scope Choice Affect Budget?

Scope is the single largest budget driver. Security-only scope requires approximately 60 to 80 controls and $15,000 to $30,000 for audit fees. Adding Confidentiality adds 15 to 20 controls and increases audit fees by 15% to 25%. Adding Availability adds another 15 to 20 controls and comparable audit cost increase. Processing Integrity and Privacy each add similar control and cost increments.

Companies pursuing four or five trust service criteria face first-year costs of $60,000 to $100,000 — nearly double the Security-only budget. The scope decision should be driven by customer requirements, not compliance ambition. Over-scoping increases both first-year and ongoing costs with no enterprise sales benefit if customers do not require the additional criteria.

## What Variable Costs Should Series A Companies Anticipate?

Beyond the fixed budget categories, Series A companies should reserve $5,000 to $15,000 for variable costs that emerge during the certification process: control remediation costs if gap analysis reveals missing tools or configurations, compliance consultant time for unexpected auditor requests, additional penetration testing if initial tests reveal high-severity findings requiring re-testing, and legal review costs for data processing agreements and vendor contracts affected by scope changes.

## FAQ

### Should we budget for Type I or Type II first?

Budget for Type II directly if your goal is enterprise sales enablement. Type I adds $8,000 to $15,000 and 2 to 3 months without satisfying most enterprise procurement requirements. The exception is very early-stage companies needing interim documentation for initial prospect conversations.

### Is the SOC 2 budget a one-time or recurring cost?

SOC 2 is a recurring annual cost. Year 2 costs typically drop 30% to 40% from Year 1 because penetration testing is the only new expense — the platform and audit are renewals rather than new purchases.
