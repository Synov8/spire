E-commerce AI systems face moderate AI Act obligations — a 2026 European Commission digital commerce report found that approximately 35% of AI systems used in EU e-commerce qualify as limited or minimal risk, but certain use cases including credit-based purchasing, biometric payment verification, and manipulative pricing AI face higher scrutiny. The AI Act interacts with existing e-commerce regulations including the Digital Services Act and Consumer Rights Directive. This guide covers EU AI Act compliance for e-commerce companies.

## What E-Commerce AI Systems Are Affected?

AI systems in e-commerce span risk levels: high-risk — creditworthiness AI for buy-now-pay-later, biometric payment verification, and AI recruiting for marketplace staff. Limited risk — customer service chatbots (transparency required), and product recommendation engines. Minimal risk — inventory management AI, logistics optimization, and marketing analytics. Most e-commerce AI falls into limited or minimal risk categories, reducing compliance burden.

## What Product Recommendation AI Requirements Apply?

Product recommendation systems are generally limited risk, requiring: transparency that the customer is interacting with an AI system, disclosure of AI-generated recommendations, opt-out options for personalized recommendations, and explainability of AI-driven recommendations where they significantly affect customer choice. Recommendations that exploit consumer vulnerabilities (targeting children, exploiting financial distress) could face increased scrutiny under prohibited AI practices.

## How Do Dynamic Pricing AI Systems Need to Comply?

Dynamic pricing AI faces specific obligations: no personalized pricing based on protected characteristics (discrimination is prohibited), transparency about AI-driven pricing, no manipulative pricing practices that could cause consumer harm, compliance with existing consumer protection law on pricing, and explainable pricing algorithms for regulatory review. The AI Act's prohibition on manipulative AI practices directly applies to e-commerce pricing AI.

## What Customer Service Chatbot Requirements Apply?

Chatbots trigger AI Act transparency obligations: customers must be informed they are interacting with an AI, not a human. Chatbots must not impersonate humans. Chatbot-generated content should be identifiable. Customers must be able to request human interaction. Chatbots must not exploit vulnerable customers. Chatbot training data must not include prohibited content. These requirements apply from August 2025 under the AI Act's transparency provisions.

## What Prohibited Practices Affect E-Commerce?

Prohibited AI practices relevant to e-commerce include: AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities (age, disability, financial situation) to distort consumer behavior. Social scoring for e-commerce platforms (denying service based on social behavior). Manipulative AI that could cause significant harm. Real-time biometric surveillance in physical retail spaces. Biometric categorization systems based on sensitive characteristics. E-commerce companies should audit their AI against these prohibitions immediately.

## How Does the AI Act Coordinate with the Digital Services Act?

The DSA and AI Act create overlapping obligations for e-commerce platforms: the DSA requires transparency about algorithmic recommendations — the AI Act adds AI-specific transparency. The DSA requires risk assessment for systemic risks — the AI Act requires AI-specific risk management for high-risk AI. The DSA requires clear labeling of advertising — the AI Act requires transparency of AI-generated content. Coordinate compliance programs to avoid duplication. Use your DSA compliance framework as a foundation for AI Act obligations.

## What Enforcement Timeline Should E-Commerce Follow?

Immediately: audit AI inventory for prohibited practices and cease any that qualify. By August 2025: implement transparency for all customer-facing AI (chatbots, AI-generated content). By August 2026: high-risk AI (credit AI, biometric payment AI) must complete conformity assessments. E-commerce companies with primarily low-risk AI face minimal compliance burden but must still ensure transparency and no prohibited practices. Document your AI inventory and risk classification as a foundational activity.
