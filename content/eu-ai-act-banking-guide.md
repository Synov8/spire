The EU AI Act imposes specific requirements on AI systems used in banking — a 2026 Deloitte banking AI survey found that 73% of AI systems deployed in EU banks will be classified as high-risk under the AI Act, requiring conformity assessments and technical documentation. Banking AI use cases including credit scoring, fraud detection, and algorithmic trading all fall into high-risk categories that require significant compliance investment. This guide covers EU AI Act compliance for the banking sector.

## What Banking AI Systems Are Classified as High-Risk?

High-risk AI systems in banking include: creditworthiness assessment (refusal of credit, credit limits), insurance pricing and underwriting, fraud detection and anti-money laundering, algorithmic trading and investment advice, customer risk profiling, and biometric identity verification for account access. These systems require conformity assessments, risk management, technical documentation, human oversight, and transparency obligations.

## What Creditworthiness AI Requirements Apply?

AI systems used to evaluate creditworthiness are classified as high-risk. Requirements include: technical documentation describing the AI model, training data, and performance metrics. Risk management system throughout the AI lifecycle. Human oversight mechanisms for credit decisions. Transparency about AI-based credit decisions with meaningful explanations. Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity measures. Compliance with existing credit regulations (CRR, Consumer Credit Directive) remains applicable alongside AI Act requirements.

## How Do Fraud Detection AI Systems Need to Comply?

Fraud detection AI systems face a nuanced classification — anti-money laundering AI may qualify for a law enforcement exception in limited circumstances, but most fraud detection AI remains high-risk. Requirements include: accurate and representative training data, bias monitoring and mitigation, explainable AI features for transaction flags, human review of automated fraud decisions, and technical documentation of model performance. Coordinate AI Act compliance with existing AML/CFT regulatory obligations.

## What Algorithmic Trading Compliance Is Required?

Algorithmic trading AI systems are high-risk and must comply with: risk management system covering market impact, technical failures, and erroneous trading. Human oversight with kill-switch capabilities. Monitoring and logging of all trading decisions. Transparency about AI-driven trading strategies. Compliance with MiFID II algorithmic trading requirements. Testing in simulation environments before deployment. Documentation of training data, model architecture, and performance metrics.

## How Does the AI Act Intersect with Existing Banking Regulations?

The AI Act supplements rather than replaces existing banking regulations (CRR, CRD, MiFID II, PSD2, AMLD). Where requirements overlap, the more stringent requirement applies. Many AI Act requirements (risk management, documentation, human oversight) already exist in banking regulations — leverage existing compliance frameworks. Update your ICAAP, risk management framework, and compliance monitoring to incorporate AI Act requirements. Notify your competent authority of high-risk AI deployment.

## What Enforcement Timeline Should Banks Follow?

The AI Act phases in: February 2025: prohibited AI practices take effect. August 2025: transparency obligations for general-purpose AI. August 2026: high-risk AI system rules for Annex III systems including banking use cases. August 2027: high-risk rules for Annex I systems. Banks should: immediately identify and inventory all AI systems, classify each AI system for risk level, begin conformity assessments for high-risk systems, and plan technical documentation updates for the August 2026 deadline.
