No SaaS company's security program perfectly matches every customer's questionnaire requirements — a 2026 Gartner vendor risk survey found that the average enterprise security questionnaire contains 15-25% questions about controls not implemented by the vendor. How you handle these discrepancies determines whether a deal advances or stalls. This guide covers a structured approach to resolving security questionnaire discrepancies.

## How Should You Analyze Questionnaire Gaps?

When a customer asks about a control you don't have: determine whether the control is required for compliance (is it in your SOC 2 scope?), assess the risk of not having the control, identify compensating controls you do have, estimate the effort to implement the control, consider how important the deal is, and decide whether to commit to implementation. Document each gap with this analysis for your response.

## What Compensating Controls Can Address Common Gaps?

Common gaps and compensating controls: customer requests dedicated security team — compensate with 24/7 SOC monitoring and a vCISO arrangement. Customer requests 7-year log retention — compensate with 365-day active retention and annual archival. Customer requests employee background checks — compensate with security training and access controls. Customer requests dedicated penetration testing — compensate with automated scanning and quarterly manual testing. Customer requests multi-region redundancy — compensate with rapid failover procedures. Compensating controls should be documented and defensible.

## When Should You Accept Risk vs Implement Controls?

Accept risk when: the control doesn't address a material risk to the customer data, compensating controls provide equivalent protection, the effort to implement exceeds the business value, or the control would degrade user experience without meaningful security benefit. Implement when: the control addresses a material risk, multiple customers ask for the same control, the control aligns with your compliance roadmap, or the deal value justifies the investment.

## How Do You Negotiate Deviations with Customers?

Negotiation strategies: explain your risk-based approach — "We chose not to implement [control] because our risk assessment showed [compensating control] provides equivalent protection at lower operational cost." Offer a phased approach — "We're implementing [control] in Q3 as part of our compliance roadmap." Provide evidence of compensating controls. Offer additional reporting or transparency in lieu of full control implementation.

## What Documentation Should You Maintain for Each Discrepancy?

For each discrepancy: the specific customer requirement, your current state, gap analysis with risk assessment, compensating controls documentation, your decision (accept, implement, or negotiate), customer response and agreement, implementation timeline if applicable, and approval from compliance lead for risk acceptance. Maintain this documentation in your deal tracking or compliance platform.

## How Do You Revisit Discrepancy Decisions Over Time?

Review discrepancy decisions quarterly: have more customers requested the same control? Is the control now required for your industry? Has the control become easier to implement? Is the control now part of your SOC 2 scope? Was the compensating control validated in your last audit? Update your questionnaire response templates based on recurring discrepancies. Turn frequently requested controls into roadmap items.
