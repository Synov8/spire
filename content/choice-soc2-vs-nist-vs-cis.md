SOC 2, NIST CSF, and CIS Controls serve different purposes — a 2026 Gartner security framework survey found that 71% of organizations use at least two security frameworks, with 34% using three or more for comprehensive coverage. Understanding which framework(s) to adopt depends on your compliance requirements, security maturity goals, and customer expectations. This guide compares SOC 2, NIST CSF, and CIS Controls to help you choose.

## What Is the Purpose of Each Framework?

SOC 2 is a compliance framework — it's designed to provide assurance to customers and auditors that controls are operating effectively. SOC 2 is about demonstrating compliance. NIST CSF is a risk management framework — it's designed to help organizations understand and improve their cybersecurity posture. NIST CSF is about managing cybersecurity risk. CIS Controls are technical controls — they're a prioritized set of actions to defend against known cyber attacks. CIS Controls are about implementation guidance.

## How Do Costs and Effort Compare?

SOC 2 Type II: $15K-$40K audit fees, 6-12 months, ongoing annual audits. NIST CSF: no certification cost (self-assessment), 3-6 months for initial assessment, ongoing continuous improvement. CIS Controls: no certification cost (self-assessment), 2-4 months for initial implementation, ongoing maintenance. SOC 2 has the highest mandatory costs. NIST and CIS implementation costs are primarily internal labor.

## Which Is Best for Your SaaS Company?

SOC 2 is essential for B2B SaaS — customers require it for procurement. NIST CSF is recommended for all organizations as a risk management framework — use it to structure your security program improvement. CIS Controls are recommended for technical security teams — use them as an implementation guide for specific controls. Many companies use all three: SOC 2 for customer compliance, NIST CSF for risk management, and CIS Controls for technical implementation guidance.

## How Much Control Overlap Exists?

SOC 2, NIST CSF, and CIS Controls share approximately 75% control overlap at the objective level. Common controls across all three: access control and identity management, awareness and training, data security, information protection processes, maintenance, protective technology, incident response, and recovery planning. SOC 2 adds trust service criteria-specific controls. NIST CSF adds supply chain and business environment considerations. CIS Controls add specific technical implementation guidance.

## Can You Use Multiple Frameworks Together?

Yes — a combined approach works well: adopt NIST CSF as your overarching risk management and security improvement framework. Use CIS Controls as your technical implementation guide for specific controls. Use SOC 2 as your customer-facing compliance certification. Map all three frameworks to a common control set. This approach provides comprehensive security coverage while meeting customer compliance requirements.

## What Compliance Strategy Works Best?

Recommended strategy: implement CIS Controls first (specific technical actions), structure your security program with NIST CSF (risk management approach), and validate compliance through SOC 2 audit (customer-facing certification). This layered approach ensures you're not just compliant on paper but actually secure. Most SaaS companies should start with SOC 2 for customer requirements, then layer NIST and CIS for program maturity.
