The EU AI Act establishes a graduated penalty structure with the highest fines in technology regulation history — up to €35 million or 7% of total worldwide annual turnover for violations involving prohibited AI practices under Article 5. The European Commission allocated €50 million for AI Act enforcement in 2026, with each member state required to designate at least one notifying authority and one market surveillance authority to enforce the regulation.

## What Are the Three Penalty Tiers?

The EU AI Act's penalty structure has three tiers based on violation type. Tier 1 (highest): Prohibited AI practices under Article 5 — deploying AI systems that deploy subliminal techniques, exploit vulnerabilities, evaluate or predict criminal offenses based solely on profiling, or create facial recognition databases through untargeted scraping — face fines of up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.

Tier 2: Non-compliance with high-risk AI system obligations (data governance, technical documentation, risk management, transparency, human oversight) faces fines of up to €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover. Tier 3: Providing incorrect or misleading information to notified bodies or national authorities faces fines of up to €7.5 million or 1.5% of global annual turnover.

## What Corrective Powers Do National Authorities Have?

National supervisory authorities have a graduated set of corrective powers: orders to bring non-compliant AI systems into compliance within a specified period, orders to withdraw AI systems from the market, orders to recall AI systems from end users, and orders to make non-compliant AI systems available for investigation.

The European AI Board coordinates cross-border enforcement. A company established in one member state can face enforcement action from any member state where its AI systems are used.

## When Will Enforcement Begin for Each Obligation?

The AI Act's graduated timeline is: February 2025 — prohibited AI practices and AI literacy obligations became enforceable. August 2025 — obligations for general-purpose AI models took effect. August 2026 — all remaining obligations for high-risk AI systems become enforceable, including the full conformity assessment requirements.

## How Should Companies Prepare for Enforcement?

Conduct an AI system inventory identifying all AI systems in use, classify each system under the AI Act's risk categories, implement AI literacy training programs, establish transparency and disclosure procedures, and document compliance evidence for high-risk systems.

## FAQ

### Does the AI Act apply to companies established outside the EU?

Yes. The AI Act has extraterritorial effect. Providers placing AI systems on the EU market and deployers whose AI output is used in the EU are subject to the Act regardless of their establishment location.

### Can fines be imposed for multiple violations simultaneously?

Yes. Each violation can attract a separate penalty, with national authorities determining the aggregate fine based on the totality of violations and the company's cooperation level.
